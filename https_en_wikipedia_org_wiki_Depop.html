Depop is a peer-to-peer social shopping app based in London, with additional offices in Manchester, Milan and New York City. However, the app has an expanding global presence being popularised in countries such as The United States, United Kingdom, Australia, New Zealand, and Italy. This app allows for users to buy and sell their own clothing and accessories. Many users utilise the app as an online thrift store.

History[edit]

Depop was founded in 2011 by entrepreneur Simon Beckerman at an Italian technological incubator and business (H-FARM) start-up centre.[1] Paolo Barberis[2] with Nana Bianca was one of the first investors in 2012 with a seed investment. Its headquarters were moved to London in 2012.[3] Depop expanded and opened additional offices in Milan and New York City.[4] Beckerman raised €1 million in funding in October 2013 from Red Circle Investment and brought on Danish serial entrepreneur Runar Reistrup as new CEO.[3] In 2015, Depop secured another investment of $8 million from Balderton Capital and Holtzbrinck Ventures.[4] Most active users of Depop are located in the United Kingdom, Australia, United States and Italy. In March 2016, former CEO, Runar Reistrup, stated that Depop's growth was achieved through word of mouth.[5]

In June 2019, Depop raised $62 million in Series C from General Atlantic. Previous investors HV Holtzbrinck Ventures, Balderton Capital, Creandum, Octopus Ventures, TempoCap and Sebastian Siemiatkowski also participated.[6]

In 2020, Depop's gross merchandise sales and revenue both more than doubled, to about $650 million and $70 million respectively. [7] On June 2, 2021, Etsy announced it would buy Depop for $1.6 billion in a "Gen Z" push.[8]

About[edit]

Depop provides a mobile marketplace that enables individuals to buy and resell their items on iOS and Android platforms.[9] It was announced in May 2016 that Depop was the official app sponsor of the 2016 Vans Warped Tour.[10] The application has attracted celebrities to resell their own items, with some donating proceeds to charitable causes.[11] Depop is especially popular with the Millennial and Gen Z audience, with 90% of users estimated to be under the age of 26,[12] because it provides a way for them to recycle unwanted items for money.[13] Depop displays a layout which is similar to that of Instagram, giving it a social media feel.

More than 32 million items are available for sale on the platform, with up to 140,000 new listings posted every day. [14]

Features[edit]

  • Interactions: The application allows users like and comment on items, as well as direct message sellers about any queries relating to the item on sale. Users may enquire about postage, how the item fits, and negotiate prices.
  • Profile page: This acts similar to that of Instagram wherein users can browse a sellers page with images of products / uploads. Profile pages also have bios, profile pictures, prices, active status, reviews, and followers / following.
  • Listing Items: To list an item, sellers are required to include relevant information including a description, hashtags (which are limited to 5), colour of item, aesthetic of item (eg. Designer, 60s. Chic, Indie, Vintage), sizing, condition (new, like new and used), and the price.
  • Depop feed: Again the feed is similar to Instagram models wherein posts are shown with times / dates of listing and utilise algorithms that aid to display what buyers may be interested in. The Explore page is also useful for buyers to find certain items or brands.
  • Payment: Payment is connected to PayPal ensuring buyer and seller protection. When a sale is made, 10% of the sale goes to Depop and 90% to the seller. Moreover, payments processor Paypal takes an additional 2.9% and $0.20 per transaction.
  • Top sellers: Depop awards individuals on the platform who achieve consistent high sale volumes to obtain Top Seller Status.[15] These users receive benefits such as a blue tick verification status, an account manager at Depop, extra consideration for explore content and exclusive access to the platform private forum.

Criticism[edit]

Depop's popularity is attributed to its push to support sustainable fashion sources. Recent discussions about the platform have pointed towards its contribution to the gentrification of charity shops.[16] For example, Depop users looking to turn a profit may purchase the most valuable items from charity shops and thus deny those who rely on the affordability of these shops the opportunity to take these items. Further, Depop users may also purchase many plus-sized items with the intent to alter and resell them, thus denying those who rely on charity shops the opportunity to find affordable items in their size.

In November 2019, Business of Fashion reported that users within the Depop app were receiving sexually suggestive messages.[17]
