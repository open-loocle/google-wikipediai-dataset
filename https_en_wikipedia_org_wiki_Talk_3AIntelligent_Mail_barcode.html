Proposed Edits to Intelligent Mail Barcode[edit]

I have been working with Pitney Bowes to improve and expand this article. We would like to propose the following edits. Please read below for edits that will be added before the content currently found on this page.

The Intelligent Mail® barcode is a 65-bar code applied to mail in the United States, which provides information and benefits to both mailers and postal officials.

The Intelligent Mail initiative was announced by the US Postal Service in 2003, became available in 2006, was expanded upon and enhanced in 2007. This barcode will be required starting in May 2009 for companies looking to earn the maximum USPS automation discounts.

The Intelligent Mail barcode replaces the POSTNETTM and PLANET® barcodes and promises to raise the level of service the USPS provides to its customers. This mandate will help the USPS improve deliverability, provide new service and increase overall efficiency.

Over the years, the Intelligent Mail barcode has also been referred to as OneCodeTM, IMB and the 4-State Barcode.


The Intelligent Mail data payload


The Intelligent Mail barcode is a height-modulated barcode that encodes up to 31-digits of mailpiece data into 65 vertical bars.


The code is made up of four distinct symbols, which is why this barcode was once referred to as the 4-State Customer Barcode. These are the tracker, ascender, descender, and full bar (TADF). In total, the new barcode will carry a data payload of 31 digits including the following elements.


Service Type Identifier. A value that corresponds to a particular mail class with a particular combination of service(s).


Mailer ID. A number assigned by the USPS that identifies the specific mailer or subscriber.


Sequence Number: An ID specific to this mailpiece that must remain unique for each 45-day period.


Delivery Point ZIP Code: The same data used to generate the POSTNET barcode today.


Benefits of a Multi-Service Barcode


Maximum Postal Savings As of May 2009, mailers looking to earn the maximum postal discounts will be required to implement the Full-Service Intelligent Mail barcode.


Visibility into the Mailstream By allowing you to track each mail piece individually, the Intelligent Mail barcode provides companies with the precise status if mail and an opportunity to add a level of certainty in several important ways:


1. Low-cost Proof of Mailing Via the USPS® Confirm® service, the Intelligent Mail barcode allows you to access Destination Confirm service for mere pennies, giving you advance notice about when your high-value mail pieces will reach their destinations.


2. Payment Tracking Origin Confirm service lets you see when an individual customer’s check (or response) is on the way back to you, enabling you to manage collections efforts smarter and avoid unnecessary cancellations.


3. More Efficient Customer Care With the right interface, you can provide mailstream visibility to your customer care representatives, giving them the time-sensitive intelligence needed to reduce talk time, determine whether or not late fees should be waived and provide more responsive customer service.

4. More Effective Marketing Being able to forecast delivery of individual mail pieces makes it easier to forecast the number and timing of incoming phone calls. This helps marketers ensure phone centers are staffed accordingly while minimizing waste. Marketers can also target precise in-home delivery dates.


Free Address Change Service The USPS is looking to reduce Undeliverable As Addressed (UAA) mail, so it’s not surprising that the new Intelligent Mail barcode makes it easy for mailers to make address corrections, as needed. In fact, on First-Class Mail®, electronic Address Change Service (ACSTM) is free when companies use the Full-Service Intelligent Mail® barcode.


Preparation Efficiency The USPS offers seamless acceptance for the induction of mailers’ prepared (presorted) mail. Whether preparing their own mail or using presort houses, mailers will benefit from reduced paperwork and greater visibility and transparency with the USPS.

Unlike the POSTNET barcode, which was a modified binary-coded decimal encoding and could be decoded by eye without too much trouble, the algorithm for encoding the intelligent mail barcode is ...complicated. The section of the official spec describing how to encode them is eight dense pages long, and involves a nonstandard conversion of decimal to binary (one digit is limited to 0-4, and the encoding takes advantage), calculating a cyclic redundancy check, dividing by 636 and capturing the remainder, repeatedly dividing by 1365 and capturing the remainder, and then looking up the remainders in a table to convert to a binary string. Each bit in this binary string corresponds to an ascender or descender in the barcode, but they're not mapped in any sensible order. I'd question the sanity of anyone who tried encoding or decoding these things by hand. While we do include a high-level description of the algorithm in this article, I don't think it's reasonable to include enough detail to implement an encoder/decoder.
On a side note, I pity anyone who's trying to implement this algorithm; it feels to me like the designer(s) were intentionally making it difficult to implement correctly. Dwarfkingdom's note about adding the history and motivations behind the design choices would make a great addition, but I haven't been able to find any reliable sources (or even unreliable ones). Alex Cohn (let's chat!) 18:53, 22 October 2018 (UTC)
Coding theory. It isn't complicated to make it difficult it implement, but to make reading more reliable. I don't know the numbers, but it has some redundancy to survive some bar errors. If you want to see a complicated code, look at the code used for CDs, which can survive about a 1mm radial black strip (or scratch). I thought for a short time about trying to implement it, but instead downloaded the Java jar file and used that. At least at the time, there was a 32 bit but not 64 bit version. Gah4 (talk) 11:57, 24 February 2020 (UTC)

Tracking or tracing[edit]

The PR for the IMB includes "By allowing you to track each mail piece individually." However, it appears that only the shipper or sender can track a mailpiece or package.

The IMb Tracing User Guide from January 2012 mentions the "Mail Tracking and Reporting (MT&R) website" at http://mailtracking.usps.com but that subdomain name does not work. Even if it did, it appears that only the shipper or sender would have logins. That User Guide also documents a raw data file that has tracking data but it appears to get the file you need to register an FTP server with the USPS who will then drop data files onto your server.

As it is, I'm wondering if tracking or tracing is operational today for the shipper. I dug into if I can track or track as I received a package shipped via UPS Mail Innovations Expedited. UPS delivered it to a local USPS processing center and as part of that added a sticker with the USPS IMb code. The UPS Mail Innovations tracking web site accepted their tracking number which shows the progress within the UPS network. It was also supposed to then show the USPS tracking results but that's stuck at "In Transit by Post Office" indicating that the UPS can't get the data from the USPS.

Unfortunately, the USPS documentation is vague which makes it tricky to include in the Wikipedia article. It simply states that things can be tracked or traced but does not show how to do this today.

FWIW - https://about.usps.com/publications/pub100/pub100_051.htm talks about the barcode history a bit. This looks useful to integrate into the Wikipedia article. --Marc Kupper|talk 22:03, 22 November 2019 (UTC)

IMB tracking is now called Informed Visibility and it does work. You don't have to use the ftp server, you can get the data directly from the web site, but it goes away after a few days. Unless you want to log in every day and download it, best to use the ftp server. And yes, as well as I know, only the sender can track, unlike FedEx and UPS where anyone can put the tracking number in. I signed up right during the transition from IMBtracing to IV, and there was some confusion and phone calls to get it right. (Complicated by somehow getting two accounts.) Gah4 (talk) 19:00, 12 February 2020 (UTC)
Documentation for Informed Visibility, the new name for IBM tracing, is here: https://postalpro.usps.com/InformedVisibility. Gah4 (talk) 12:02, 24 February 2020 (UTC)

Math Error in Symbology section[edit]

I believe there is an error in the Symbology section. 

 The total number of characters is two times 1365, or 2730. Log2(2730) is 11.41469. So the 65 bars (or 130 bits) encode a 114-bit message.

should be 

 The total number of characters is two times 1365, or 2730. Log2(2730) is 11.41469. So the 65 bars (or 130 bits) encode a 11.4-bit message.

changing 114-bit message to 11.4-bit message.

I'm not sure if you can really talk about a fraction of a bit like that, so maybe it's just an 11-bit message? Either way, it is definitely not 114, as 2^114 is ridiculously huge.

Also, in this case, should I just make the change? Or is it better to let the admin of the page commit the change? Thank you. Mrsuperboot (talk) 14:33, 10 July 2020 (UTC)

The math is correct. The calculation is for each of the 10 groups, which are then combined. The grouping allows the ECC to be done on groups, and then combined into the result. There is nothing especially wrong with fractional bits in information theory. A decimal digit represents about 3.32 bits, so three digits about 9.94. That math leads to densely packed decimal where 10 bits hold three decimal digits, with only 0.06 bits wasted, or 0.02 per digits. BCD wastes 0.68 bits per digit. This encoding is now used in the decimal floating point forms of IEEE_754. Gah4 (talk) 18:24, 10 July 2020 (UTC)
